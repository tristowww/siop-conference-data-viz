Reading the shift

The interesting part is not just that AI increased.

The portfolio story is about where AI shows up. Selection and assessment remain a natural home for AI because those use cases often involve prediction, validation, scoring, and structured decision support. But by 2026, AI also appears more often in work that is less rule-based and more dependent on context, judgment, learning, and employee experience.

Meaning 4 of 5

I-side and O-side

Why the context shift matters.

In I-O psychology, the I-side often centers individual measurement and selection. The O-side often centers organizations, teams, culture, learning, and work experience. AI can support both, but the design questions and risks change.

Plainly: I-side is selection and assessment; O-side is teams, leadership, learning, culture, and work design.

More rule-based

I-side selection and assessment

Common AI questions include how to score, predict, validate, audit, and protect assessments. These use cases usually need clear evidence, consistency, fairness checks, and defensible decision rules.

More judgment-heavy

O-side training and organization development

Common AI questions include how to coach, teach, personalize learning, improve leadership, support teams, and redesign work. These use cases depend more on context, sensemaking, trust, and human judgment.

The clearest story is a shift from AI mainly as measurement technology toward AI as support for learning, leadership, and organizational change. The assessment conversation is still there, but it is no longer the whole story.

Risk questions

Fairness and validity vs. trust and autonomy

I-side use cases often foreground adverse impact, construct validity, transparency, cheating, and legal defensibility. O-side use cases add concerns about surveillance, psychological safety, deskilling, credibility, and whether AI changes the employee experience.

Evaluation standard

Prediction quality vs. contextual usefulness

Selection AI tends to be judged by whether it predicts job-relevant outcomes fairly and consistently. O-side AI is more likely to be judged by whether it helps people learn, reflect, decide, coordinate, or change behavior in context.

Product role

Decision automation vs. decision support

I-side tools may sit close to consequential employment decisions. O-side tools more often look like augmentation: coaching, summarizing, scenario practice, nudging, sensemaking, and personalized development.

Who is affected

Applicants vs. employees, managers, and teams

The target population changes the design problem. Candidate-facing AI raises questions about procedural justice and defensible evaluation. Employee-facing AI raises questions about consent, power, role clarity, and how work systems absorb the tool.

So what 5 of 5

So what

The sharper story is where AI is showing up.

Across the public SIOP program data, AI appears to be moving beyond a mostly assessment-and-methods conversation into a broader set of people-centered applications. Selection did not disappear. The newer pattern is that training, coaching, leadership, culture, accessibility, and work design are becoming easier to see in the AI conversation.

What changed More volume, broader use cases

More sessions mention AI, and org-side topics become easier to see in the program mix.

Why it matters The design problem changes

O-side AI is less about one score and more about judgment, trust, learning, and context.

How to read it A conference pattern, not a causal claim

The charts describe conference sessions and examples, not definitive field-wide adoption.
